Abstract Information

Those interested in presenting a poster should submit an abstract by February 28th, 2025

  • 150-300 words
  • Title: Clear and concise; reflects the main focus of the research.
  • Authors and Affiliations: Full names of all contributing authors; institutional affiliations.
  • Introduction/Background
  • Objectives: Specific aims or research questions; clear articulation of purpose.
  • Methods
  • Results
  • Conclusion/Discussion: Implications or significance of findings; connection to objectives or contribution.
  • Keywords: 3–5 relevant keywords for indexing and searching.

Schedule

Time Frame Phase Notes
7:00 - 8:00 AM Check-in / Poster Setup
8:00 - 8:30 AM Opening Statement from APSA National Leadership Committee This session will be a brief introduction to what APSA and its mission are and will be conducted by APSA central leadership.
8:30 - 9:15 AM Keynote Speaker 1: Succeeding as an Underrepresented Physician-Scientist in STEM This session will feature a keynote speaker who has demonstrated commitment to addressing DEI-related challenges and sharing their solutions to these challenges. The purpose is to provide a reference and inspiration for trainees who may face similar obstacles along their training path.
9:15 - 9:30 AM Break/Transition
9:30 - 10:30 AM Panel: Q&A with Research-Track Residency Program Directors This session will feature a Q&A panel with directors of Research-Track Residency Programs, with the purpose being increased transparency about applicant expectations, program goals, and anticipated developments within each speaker's program. Q&A will be guided by a monitor with opportunities for attendees to ask questions.
10:30 - 11:15 AM Break/Transition
11:15 - 12:15 PM Panel: Q&A with Physician Scientists in Industry This session will be a guided discussion with 4-5 representatives from industry who can speak to the challenges and opportunities of working as a physician scientist outside of academia, and share advice from their own experience in their respective fields. Time will also be allotted for attendees to ask questions.
12:15 - 1:00 PM Lunch Break
1:15 - 2:00 PM Roundtable Session: Trainees and Physician Scientists This session will host a mix of trainees and currently-practicing physician scientists at eight tables throughout the venue. Attendees can visit each table to ask questions about the representative's program, institution, and/or company.
2:00 - 2:45 PM Keynote Speaker 2: Tips for Success as a Physician Scientist This keynote will feature a highly successful physician scientist who can share insight into their career path, challenges they faced and how they overcame them, and advice for prospective and current trainees.
2:45 - 3:00 PM Break/Transition
3:00 - 5:00 PM Poster Session This two-hour session will feature both in-person and virtual posters for attendees to visit, view, and discuss with selected presenters.
5:00 - 5:15 PM Break/Transition
5:15 - 5:30 PM Closing Remarks

Gabriel Haddad, MD

Distinguished Professor of Pediatrics and Neurosciences, Vice Dean for Children’s Academic Programs and Chairman of the Department of Pediatrics at the University of California, San Diego; Physician-in-Chief, Chief Scientific Officer at Rady Children’s Hospital-San Diego

Q&A with Research-Track Residency Program Directors

Dr. Haddad received his medical education and initial medical training at the American University of Beirut in Lebanon, followed by additional training at the University of Texas in Houston. He then moved to Columbia University in New York to do his fellowship and joined the faculty. At Columbia University, he was the director of the Sleep Laboratory in the Division and trained many fellows. He started his faculty career there and advanced the field of the Sudden Infant Death Syndrome and the understanding of its pathophysiology. He was also the first to describe a disease that is now known as Haddad’s syndrome, involving poor drive to breathe (secondary to central pathology), Hirschsprung’s disease and abnormally low variability in heart rate. After 13 years at Columbia, he was recruited to Yale University to be the Division Chief of Respiratory Medicine Shortly after he became also Professor of Cellular & Molecular Physiology at the same institution. After 14 years at Yale, he was recruited to Albert Einstein College of Medicine to be the Chairman of the Department of Pediatrics and to Montefiore Hospital to be Pediatrician in Chief. In 2005, he was recruited West to become Chair of Pediatrics at the University of California San Diego and physician-in-Chief at Rady Children’s Hospital where he has been since then. Dr. Haddad has published over 400 peer-reviewed articles and chapters, and authored numerous medical texts including the landmark Basic Mechanisms of Pediatric Respiratory Disease (2002), Brain Hypoxia and Ischemia (2008), Genetic Models in Cardiorespiratory Biology (2001) and Tissue Oxygen Deprivation: From Molecular to Integrated Function (1996). He has been Chair of the Respiratory Neurobiology and Sleep Assembly of the American Thoracic Society and Chair at the NIH for a number of conferences on SIDS and Sleep, including the Chair of the Board of the National Sleep Center at the NIH. He sat on numerous national committees such as the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute and March of Dimes, and is a member of 19 distinguished medical societies, many in a leadership role. He has held numerous editorial appointments; is a reviewer for >40 respected medical journals; is the recipient of numerous awards and honors; and has been invited to speak at national and international conferences. He has mentored many graduate students, residents, fellows and junior faculty many of whom have their own laboratory, are division chiefs and others are chairs of clinical or basic science Departments. He was elected to the Association of American Physicians in 2008. He has been a member of the Board of St. Jude Children’s Research Hospital since 2011 and currently is Vice Chair of the Board of Governors.

David A. Schwartz, MD

UColorado, Program to Advance Physician Scientists & Translational Research

Q&A with Research-Track Residency Program Directors

David Schwartz is a Distinguished Professor of Medicine and Immunology and Associate Dean for Translational Science at the University of Colorado. In his role as Associate Dean, he directs a campus-wide program to promote translational and programmatic research and support the career development of physician-scientists at all stages of career development. He previously served as the Chair of Medicine at the University of Colorado and the Director of the National Institutes of Environmental Health Sciences at the NIH. He has made numerous contributions toward understanding the role of the environment and human genome in the development of complex human diseases. These efforts have provided new insights into the genetics, epigenetics, and genomics of interstitial lung disease, asthma, and innate immunity. His work has led to the recognition that genetic susceptibility, and specifically MUC5B, plays a role in the etiology of pulmonary fibrosis. Dr. Schwartz’ lab was the first to clone the human TLR4 gene and demonstrate that variation in this gene decreased immune responsiveness to endotoxin, enhanced the risk of Gram negative sepsis, and protected individuals from the development of coronary artery disease.

Manish Kohli, MD

Dr. Kohli’s responsibilities involve chairing national (NCI-NIH; CDMRP; NSF) and International study sections that involve cutting edge biotechnology focus in cancer. His research in cancer focuses on advancing innovative clinical strategies to treat relapsed or refractory patients in a multi-disciplinary setting that involves:

Fusing ultra-sensitive bio-engineering detection technologies with omic-based tumor biology.

Developing real-world biobanks and datasets to implement data sciences that lead to molecular evidence-based algorithms and clinical trial designs with the potential to impact clinical practice.

With rapid advancements in biological sciences and computing power, our ongoing multi-disciplinary teams apply novel approaches to develop in silico multi-omic, machine learning algorithms to pursue innovative biomarker-based enrichment-type clinical trial designs. Devin Wiley, MD, PhD

Engineer, Logic Technology Development Division - Intel Corporation; Co-founder, Circumvent Pharmaceuticals; Co-founder, Poynter Technologies

Q&A with Physician Scientists in Industry

Dr. Devin Wiley is an engineer and entrepreneur with expertise spanning the fields of nanotechnology, microelectronics manufacturing and development, and neurotherapeutic development. Dr. Wiley is currently a senior engineer and Head of Integrated Lithography at Intel Corporation responsible for identifying, approving, and developing final designs for state-of-the-art Artificial Intelligence-associated integrated circuit architectures. His work includes driving development and qualification of new lithography processes and tools, and preparing new technologies for transfer to High Volume Manufacturing Facilities - including most recently Intel's new 18A chip technology. Dr. Wiley heads lithography resist chemistry development for contact/holes in both the current and new high-Numeric Aperture Extreme Ultraviolet (hi-NA EUV) toolset. Dr. Wiley has combined this expertise in microelectronics manufacturing with expertise in the nanoparticle therapeutic space to founding Velanidi Technologies (now Poynter Technologies), which enables 'bio-lithographically compatible' printing of single capture agents (e.g. DNA-Antibody fragment conjugates) to individual binding pads linked to state-of-the-art integrated circuits and transistor nodes — enabling the detection and addressing of single molecular binding events on a large scale. This technology has applications to drug development, diagnostics, and potentially future therapeutics when administered as a nano-to-micron sized particles. Dr. Wiley has also spent the last ten years as a co-founder of Circumvent Pharmaceuticals, which is dedicated to developing a first therapeutic option for CLN1 Batten Disease, an ultrarare form of pediatric neurodegeneration. As a co-founder, Dr. Wiley has led financing efforts through private equities (including the Y-combinator accelerator program) and grants (through both family foundations and the NIH SBIR/STTR programs), raising nearly $10M to date. At Circumvent, Dr. Wiley leads efforts that span chemistry manufacturing and controls (CMC), drug metabolism and pharmacokinetics (DMPK), toxicology and safety pharmacology, IND-and-CTA enabling studies, and regulatory engagement including driving informal and formal meetings with the FDA/EMA toward IND/CTA submission. Dr. Wiley has served on the Clinical Advisory Board at Intel, which focused on pathfinding of microelectronics-based technologies for clinical applications. Additionally, he serves as the Chair of the Oregon Health and Sciences University Hillsboro Medical Foundation, which notably raises $1M+ per year through the 'Salud' program that provides mobile healthcare access to seasonal vineyard workers in the Willamette Valley Wine Industry. Dr. Wiley received his M.D. From the USC Keck School of Medicine, and his Ph.D. from Caltech in Chemical Engineering. Outside of work, Dr. Wiley enjoys white water rafting, playing the piano, cooking, and spending time with his wife and three children.

Carol Meltzer, MD

Dean, USC Keck School of Medicine

Keynote Speaker 2

Carolyn C. Meltzer, MD, is Dean of the Keck School of Medicine of USC and holds the May S. and John H. Hooval Dean’s Chair in Medicine. Dr. Meltzer oversees the operations and academic affairs of the Keck School, which is home to 26 basic science and clinical academic programs and 14 major research institutes. She serves as the academic leader of the Keck School faculty and staff, who perform biomedical research and clinical care, and train medical students, graduate students, postdoctoral trainees, and undergraduates. The Keck School, in collaboration with the health systems of Keck Medicine of USC and Los Angeles Medical Center, sponsors more than 1,200 resident physicians in more than 70 specialty or subspecialty programs.

Prior to joining USC, Dr. Meltzer served as the William P. Timmie Professor and Chair of the Department of Radiology and Imaging Sciences at Emory University. She also served in two additional leadership roles at Emory’s School of Medicine: Executive Associate Dean of Faculty Academic Advancement, Leadership, and Inclusion, and Chief Diversity and Inclusion Officer.

Dr. Meltzer has a lengthy history of service to the medical profession on a national level, serving on numerous professional and advisory boards. She currently serves on the Council of Deans Administrative Board for the Association of American Medical Colleges (AAMC), Board of Directors of the Radiological Society of North America, and the Board of the California Institute for Regenerative Medicine (CIRM).

A physician-scientist, Dr. Meltzer is expert in neuroradiology and nuclear medicine and has performed landmark research on the brain’s structure and function during normal aging, Alzheimer’s disease, and neuropsychiatric disorders across the lifespan. She also led the clinical evaluation of the world’s first human combined PET-CT scanner for cancer staging and treatment. Dr. Meltzer has received numerous awards and honors, including election as a fellow of the American Association for the Advancement of Science, the distinguished service award from the American Medical Association, the gold medal award from the Association of Academic Radiology, and the outstanding researcher award and gold medal from the American Society of Neuroradiology. She is a member of the Academy Council of Distinguished Investigators and gold medal awardee in the Academy for Radiology and Biomedical Imaging Research. Dr. Meltzer earned her medical degree from The Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine and completed her graduate medical education at The Johns Hopkins Hospital in Baltimore. She holds an undergraduate degree in biology and neurobiology with honors from Cornell University.

On a personal note, Dr. Meltzer’s photography has been the creative complement to her medical imaging career. Her art has been featured in more than 50 solo and group exhibits in the United States and Europe, presented in the Yale University Beinecke Library Archive,and published in South x Southeast Photomagazine, the American Journal of Neuroradiology, and Edge of Humanity Magazine.
